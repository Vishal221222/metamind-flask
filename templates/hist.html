Historiography

Historiography is the study of how history is written, interpreted, and understood through different perspectives, methods, and ideologies.

Start Learning

What is Historiography?

Historiography is not just the study of past events, but the study of how historians interpret, write, and present history. It focuses on perspectives, biases, sources, and methods used to construct historical narratives.

Interpretation

History is shaped by the historian’s viewpoint and social context.

Sources

Primary and secondary sources influence historical accuracy.

Bias

Every historical account reflects some level of selection and bias.

Major Schools of Historiography

Traditional Historiography

Focuses mainly on kings, rulers, wars, political events, dynasties, and major personalities.

Marxist Historiography

Interprets history through class struggle, economic systems, production relations, and material conditions.

Annales School

Studies long-term social structures, geography, economy, culture, and everyday life instead of only political events.

Postmodern Historiography

Questions objectivity, absolute truth, grand narratives, and the neutrality of historical writing.

Feminist Historiography

Highlights women’s roles, gender relations, patriarchy, and hidden female voices in history.

Subaltern Historiography

Focuses on marginalized groups, peasants, workers, colonized people, and voices ignored by elite history.

Historical Methods & Sources

Primary Sources

Original historical materials such as letters, diaries, inscriptions, official records, artifacts, coins, newspapers, and photographs.

Secondary Sources

Books, articles, documentaries, and research papers written by historians after analyzing primary evidence.

Oral History

Personal memories, interviews, community stories, and traditions passed from one generation to another.

Archaeology

Material remains such as tools, buildings, pottery, bones, monuments, and settlement remains used to reconstruct the past.

Comparative Analysis

Comparing different historical accounts, regions, periods, and sources to identify similarities, contradictions, and patterns.

Interdisciplinary Method

Uses sociology, economics, anthropology, geography, linguistics, archaeology, and political science to study history deeply.

Evolution Timeline

Ancient Period

Herodotus and Thucydides developed early forms of historical writing, focusing on wars, rulers, events, and human actions.

Medieval Period

Historical writing was strongly influenced by religion, divine will, chronicles, royal courts, and moral lessons.

Renaissance

Humanism encouraged historians to study classical texts, politics, culture, and human agency with renewed interest.

Enlightenment

Reason, progress, scientific thinking, and critical questioning shaped modern approaches to historical interpretation.

19th Century

Evidence-based professional history developed, with emphasis on archives, documents, chronology, and factual accuracy.

20th Century

Marxist history, Annales School, social history, economic history, and cultural history expanded the discipline.

Contemporary Era

Feminist, postmodern, subaltern, environmental, global, and digital history introduced new voices and methods.

Important Historians

Herodotus

Known as the “Father of History”. He used storytelling, travel accounts, and inquiry to describe past events.

Thucydides

Introduced a more analytical, political, and evidence-based style of history writing.

Leopold von Ranke

Promoted archival research and objective history based on primary sources.

Karl Marx

Developed historical materialism, interpreting history through economic structures and class struggle.

Fernand Braudel

Associated with the Annales School and long-term historical structures.

Ranajit Guha

Founder of Subaltern Studies, focusing on marginalized and non-elite historical voices.

Why Historiography Matters

Understanding History Critically

Historiography helps us understand that history is not only a collection of facts. It is also a process of interpretation. By studying historiography, we learn how narratives are created, whose voices are included, whose voices are ignored, and how power affects historical memory.

Critical Thinking: It teaches us to question historical claims.

Source Analysis: It helps us judge the reliability of evidence.

Multiple Perspectives: It shows that the same event can have different interpretations.

Social Awareness: It brings attention to ignored communities and hidden histories.
